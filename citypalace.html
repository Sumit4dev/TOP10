City Palace
View of the Chandra Mahal at city palace
The history of the City Palace is deeply intertwined with that of Jaipur and its rulers, beginning with Maharaja Sawai Jai Singh II, who reigned from 1699 to 1744. He is credited with initiating the palace complex's construction by building its extensive outer wall. Originally ruling from his capital in Amber, located about 11 kilometers (6.8 miles) from Jaipur, he relocated the capital to Jaipur in 1727 due to population growth and water scarcity. Jai Singh planned Jaipur in six blocks separated by broad avenues, using principles of Vastushastra and similar classical texts. He worked under the architectural guidance of Vidyadar Bhattacharya, a Bengali architect from Naihati in present-day West Bengal, who began as an accounts clerk in Amberâ€™s treasury and was later appointed Chief Architect by the king.
After Jai Singh's death in 1744, internal conflicts arose among the Rajput rulers in the region, though they maintained cordial relations with the British Raj. Maharaja Ram Singh supported the British during the Sepoy Mutiny of 1857, aligning himself with the imperial powers. He is credited with painting the entire city of Jaipur, including its monuments (such as the City Palace), in a distinct stucco "Pink." Since then, Jaipur has been known as the "Pink City." This color transformation was made as a gesture of hospitality to honor the Prince of Wales (who later became King Edward VII) during his visit, and the color scheme has since become an iconic symbol of Jaipur.
